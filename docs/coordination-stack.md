# The Coordination Stack: From Local Workshop to Bioregional Intelligence

A narrative overview of how the pieces compose — from a single mapping workshop to federated commitment pools, threshold-based capital flows, and AI-navigable bioregional knowledge.

---

## The Thread

Start with one person in a room in Victoria.

It's a spring morning, maybe May 2026. Fifteen people are sitting in a circle in a community hall in the Greater Victoria landscape — watershed stewards, food systems organizers, Indigenous land defenders, restoration practitioners, community gardeners. They've been invited to a bioregional mapping workshop. The facilitator asks:

*"What can you offer to this community's regeneration work? And what do you need that you can't currently get?"*

One person says: "I can offer twenty hours of Garry Oak habitat restoration — I have the tools and I know the sites."

Another says: "I need someone who understands riparian restoration in the Saanich Peninsula. I have the land but not the knowledge."

These two people may or may not find each other in the room. But they've just made the most important move in a coordination system that extends far beyond this workshop.

This document traces that thread — from the workshop floor to the bioregion, from the bioregion to the global commons, and back.

---

## Layer 1: The Workshop as Sensing

The mapping workshop is where the landscape looks at itself.

Five parts unfold across three or four hours:

- **Who's here** — every person, organization, project, and place gets named
- **What's happening** — the group maps the landscape: what exists, what's connected, where the gaps are
- **What matters most** — the gap between where we are and where we want to be becomes visible
- **Offers and needs** — each person articulates what they can contribute and what they require
- **Commitments** — concrete pledges, as specific as possible

This is a **sensing** event. The landscape becomes aware of itself through the people who inhabit and care for it. The gap between Parts 2 and 3 — between what IS and what SHOULD BE — is the engine. That tension is what generates the energy for action.

But sensing is only valuable if the signal is captured, structured, and remembered.

---

## Layer 2: The Knowledge Graph as Memory

After the workshop, the recording goes through a structured pipeline:

1. **Local transcription** (MacWhisper, no cloud upload)
2. **Human review**: the stewards (Darren and Polly in Victoria's case) structure the outputs: entities, relationships, offers, needs, commitments. Nothing enters the knowledge base without both reviewing it.
3. **Approved items enter the knowledge graph**

What the graph holds after this process is not a document. It's a living structured record:

- **People** — who was there, what they care about, what they offered, what they need
- **Organizations** — which groups are active, what they're working on, where
- **Locations** — specific places, watersheds, ecosystems, neighborhoods
- **Offers** — structured: who, what, how much, timeframe, constraints
- **Needs** — structured: who, what, urgency, whether cash or in-kind can close it
- **Commitments** — concrete pledges with lifecycle states: `PROPOSED → VERIFIED → ACTIVE → FULFILLED → EVIDENCE`

This graph lives in the Greater Victoria (GV) node — one leaf node in the Bioregional Knowledge Commons network. It is sovereign to Victoria. Darren and Polly control what's visible beyond it.

The graph is not a spreadsheet. It's queryable, updatable, federable. It holds relational memory across seasons — not just what was offered in spring 2026, but what was fulfilled, what wasn't, what the landscape learned.

## Layer 3: The Commitment Pool as Circulation

A knowledge graph of offers and needs is useful. But it's still passive — a record of what exists, not a mechanism for putting it to work.

This is where commitment pooling enters.

A commitment pool is a **shared field where individual offers and needs compose into collective capacity**. Think of it like a cooperative: individually, a promise of 20 hours of Garry Oak restoration is just a note. Pooled with fifteen other promises — watershed monitoring, food systems knowledge, equipment loans, facilitation skills, seed saving, grant writing — it becomes something the community can draw on, route, and redeem.

The mechanics:

- Each offer becomes a **voucher**: a typed, bounded commitment with lifecycle states
- Vouchers are verified by the community (not just declared — evidence of capacity matters)
- Once verified, they're poolable: routable to where they're needed
- When redeemed, they generate **evidence**: proof of fulfillment, linked back to the original offer and need

The evidence closes the loop. The landscape's knowledge of what works updates. The next mapping workshop starts from a richer picture.

What Will Ruddick of Grassroots Economics calls "the long tail of pooling" is this:

> *"You can think of the long tail of pooling being that we all express our agreements and the network of overlapping pools is the fully intangible un-ownable commons."*

When enough communities do this, the **overlapping network of pools becomes the commons itself** — not owned by anyone, not controlled by anyone, but navigable by everyone who participates.

Victoria's pool connects to Cowichan Valley's pool. Cowichan connects to Saltspring. The Salish Sea landscape groups connect to each other. Then to Olympic Peninsula, to Cascadia, to other bioregions.

The Garry Oak restoration offer made in that Victoria workshop room can — with the right trust links and routing — find the person in Cowichan who needs exactly that knowledge.

## Layer 4: Flow Funding as Redistribution

Commitment pools handle what communities can offer each other in-kind. But some needs require capital — money that has to come from outside the pool.

Flow funding enters here. Two complementary mechanisms operate on top of the knowledge graph:

### Hub Cultivator — Trust-Based Stewardship

Regenerate Cascadia's model. A trusted steward (Darren and Polly in Victoria, another pair in each landscape group) receives a capital allocation from the program ($3,000 in Phase 3). Their allocation decisions are informed by what the mapping workshop surfaced:

- Which needs are unmet by the commitment pool?
- Which offers could be activated with a small injection of capital?
- Where is the largest gap between where we are and where we want to be?

Every decision is documented: recipient, amount, need addressed, rationale. The decision feeds back into the knowledge graph as an Evidence entity. The loop closes: **need → commitment → capital → work → outcome → evidence → learning**.

Kinship Earth's four reflective questions govern the reporting: *What inspired you? What surprised you? What challenged you? What moved you?* Narrative, not metrics. Trust, not compliance.

### TBFF — Threshold-Based Algorithmic Redistribution

The Mycopunks / MycoFi model. Each participant sets two thresholds:
- A **needs threshold**: the minimum they require to participate without hardship
- A **comfort level**: the maximum they'll hold before overflow redistributes

When capital flows to someone above their comfort level, the excess automatically routes to those below their needs threshold — algorithmically, on-chain, with full provenance. The algorithm iterates until the network reaches equilibrium.

The intuition: **capital flows like water through a watershed** — from pools that are full to tributaries that are dry, governed by trust relationships and thresholds set by participants themselves, not institutional gatekeepers.

**These are not competing mechanisms.** They're complementary metabolic functions at different scales and time horizons:

| Mechanism | Governance | Scale | Timing |
|-----------|-----------|-------|--------|
| **Hub Cultivator** | Human steward judgment, informed by knowledge graph | Landscape group | Seasonal allocation cycles |
| **TBFF** | Algorithmic overflow, participant-set thresholds | Network | Continuous streaming |

Both read from the knowledge graph (evidence for decisions) and write back to it (allocation outcomes as Evidence entities with provenance). The graph is the shared memory that makes both mechanisms trustworthy.

## Layer 5: AI Agents as the Nervous System

A bioregional knowledge commons with federated nodes, commitment pools, and flow funding is powerful but not easy to navigate. This is where AI agents enter — not as the center of the system, but as the interface layer that makes the graph legible and usable.

The Octo AI agent (the Salish Sea coordinator node) can answer questions like:

- *"What needs does the Cowichan Valley group have that match what Victoria has offered?"*
- *"Who in the Cascadia network has verifiable experience with Garry Oak restoration?"*
- *"What evidence exists for successful watershed monitoring commitments in the past two years?"*

These are not search queries returning documents. They're graph traversals across federated nodes — spanning Victoria's GV node, the Salish Sea coordinator, and potentially other bioregion nodes — synthesized by an agent that knows the ontology and can reason across entity relationships.

The agents also assist with the mapping process itself: transcript processing, entity extraction, relationship identification, offer and need structuring. They make the pipeline from workshop → knowledge graph faster and more consistent, while keeping human review mandatory before anything enters the commons.

As the network grows — more landscape groups, more nodes, more commitments — the agents become more valuable. They serve the matchmaking function that no individual steward has the bandwidth to perform across a whole bioregion.

What's critical is the separation of concerns: **the agents don't own the knowledge. The communities do.** The agents serve the knowledge commons; they don't supplant it. Every entity in the graph was put there by a human who reviewed it. Every commitment was verified by a community.

## Layer 6: Federation as the Bioregional Structure

The system is holonic: each level of organization is sovereign at its own scale, nested within the level above.

```
Greater Victoria (leaf node)
  └── Salish Sea (coordinator node)
        └── Cascadia (meta-coordinator)
              └── Turtle Island (network-of-networks)
```

Each node:
- Runs its own knowledge graph (PostgreSQL + pgvector), its own database, its own identity
- Controls its own consent and visibility settings — `public`, `authorized`, or `node_private`
- Decides which peers it federates with (edge-approval governance, no unknown handshakes)
- Signs events cryptographically before sharing

What travels between nodes is **signals, not data dumps**. A community in the Olympic Peninsula doesn't receive Victoria's full database. They receive what Victoria has explicitly chosen to share, translated through the BKC meta-protocol, with provenance and consent metadata attached.

This is the cosmolocal principle in practice: **light protocols shared globally, heavy relationships held locally.** The protocol is shared (KOI, BKC ontology, meta-protocol invariants). The knowledge is sovereign.

## Layer 7: Bioregional Finance as the Capital Layer

The coordination stack described so far — workshops, knowledge graphs, commitment pools, flow funding, AI agents, federation — operates mostly in the realm of relationships, knowledge, and in-kind exchange. But regeneration work requires capital. Land restoration costs money. Species recovery programs need sustained funding. Community-owned infrastructure requires investment.

This is where **Bioregional Financing Facilities (BFFs)** enter.

A BFF is not a bank in the conventional sense. It is a **semi-permeable membrane** — the connective tissue between the global capital system (with its trillions of dollars, its ESG mandates, its search for "impact") and the ground-level regenerators who actually know how to heal a watershed, restore a Garry Oak meadow, or rebuild a food system. The membrane is designed to let capital in on terms that serve the bioregion rather than extracting from it.

The BioFi framework identifies four types:

| Facility | Function | Instruments |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Bioregional Trust** | Catalytic grants and eco-credits for early-stage regeneration | Grants, eco-credits, bioregional tithing |
| **Venture Studio** | Incubation of bioregional enterprises and commons infrastructure | Equity, patient capital, technical assistance |
| **Investment Company** | Systemic capital at landscape scale | Regeneration bonds, systemic funds, bioregional tithing |
| **Bioregional Bank** | Lending, mutual credit, and currency issuance | Lending, complementary currency, obligation clearing |

What unifies all four is the **4 Returns framework**: every investment is measured not just by financial return, but by Inspiration (cultural vitality), Ecological (measurable ecosystem health), Social (community wellbeing), and Economic/Financial returns together. This reframes "risk" — in a conventional portfolio, a watershed that is degrading looks fine. In a 4-Returns portfolio, it is a liability.

### Nature-Based Currency: Economic Sovereignty at the Bioregional Scale

The Bioregional Bank goes further than lending. It can issue **complementary currencies** — monetary instruments that circulate within the bioregion alongside national currency, facilitating transactions that would otherwise be impossible.

The most radical version: **nature-based currency**. Rather than pegging value to gold or to sovereign debt, the currency's value tracks ecosystem health indicators — salmon population counts, river flow rates, forest carbon density, species richness. A healthy watershed literally increases the money supply. Ecosystem degradation contracts it. The economy breathes with the land.

This is not a metaphor. Grassroots Economics has built functioning commitment pools and community currencies at scale (26,367 users, 188 active pools in Kenya). The tools exist. What the BioFi framework adds is the institutional layer — the governance, the legal structures, the capital stack — that can bring these tools to bioregional scale without losing their community-rooted character.

### How BFFs Connect to the Rest of the Stack

BFFs are not separate from the coordination stack described in Layers 1–6. They read from it and write back to it:

- **Reads from the knowledge graph**: The mapping workshop surfaces unmet needs. The BFF Trust sees these as grant-eligible opportunities. The Investment Company sees long-horizon regeneration projects. The Bank sees latent capacity that can be activated with credit.
- **Reads from commitment pools**: Pooled offers and verified vouchers are evidence of community capacity — the foundation for credit underwriting. A bioregion with 200 verified commitments and strong evidence of fulfillment is a better credit risk than one without.
- **Reads from flow funding**: Hub Cultivator and TBFF decisions feed back into the knowledge graph as Evidence entities with provenance. BFFs can trace capital flows from allocation to outcome — the audit trail that impact investors require.
- **Writes back to the knowledge graph**: Every grant decision, every investment, every currency issuance generates new Evidence entities. The capital flows become part of the bioregion's memory — what worked, what didn't, what needed capital vs. what needed coordination.

### The Capital Stack

Not all capital is the same. The BioFi framework distinguishes a spectrum from **concessional** (grants, patient capital, first-loss tranches) to **market-rate** (bonds, equity, impact investment) — with each layer enabling the next:

```
Concessional ──────────────────────────────────── Market-rate
  Grants        Eco-credits    Regeneration bonds    Equity
  (Bioregional   (Bioregional   (Investment Co.)    (Venture Studio)
   Trust)         Trust)
                    ↑
         Nature-based currencies
         (Bioregional Bank)
```

The concessional capital de-risks the ecosystem. Once there is evidence of impact (from the knowledge graph), market-rate capital can follow. The MRV platform — combining Traditional Ecological Knowledge, Western science, AI analysis, and blockchain verification — provides the credible evidence that unlocks this transition.

### The Salish Sea Connection

The BioFi ebook explicitly features Regenerate Cascadia as Case Study 9 — acknowledging its co-authors (Taya Seidler, Clare Attwell, Brandon Letsinger) in the acknowledgements. This is not coincidence. The Landscape Hub Cultivator program that generates the mapping workshop data, the commitment pools, and the knowledge graph — is the **ground-level infrastructure** that a Cascadian BFF would sit atop.

The coordination stack is the bioregion learning to see itself clearly. The Bioregional Financing Facility is how that self-knowledge attracts and directs capital. Neither works without the other.

## The Convergence Point: TIBC

The Turtle Island Bioregional Congress (September 14–19, 2026, Vernonia OR) — the first in 17 years — is the first moment the distributed work becomes a shared visible landscape.

Every LHC landscape group is running a mapping workshop by July 2026. By September, those outputs — entities, offers, needs, commitments — are in the bioregional knowledge network. At TIBC, for the first time, it becomes possible to see Cascadia's landscape groups in aggregate:

- Who's here across the bioregion?
- What are we collectively offering and collectively needing?
- Where are the gaps between where we are and where we want to be — at bioregional scale?
- What commitments have been made, and what evidence of fulfillment exists?

This is not a report. It's a living graph that can be queried, navigated, and added to at the congress itself. The mapping that happened in individual workshops in the spring becomes a shared bioregional picture in the fall.

What one community learned, another can build on. What one landscape group offered, another might need. The offers that didn't find matches within Victoria might find them across Cascadia.

## What This Is

This is not a platform. It's a **coordination stack** — a set of composable, sovereignty-preserving layers that communities can adopt partially or fully, on their own terms.

| Layer | What it does | Governed by |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Mapping workshop** | Sensing — landscape becomes aware of itself | Community, facilitated by stewards |
| **Knowledge graph** | Memory — structured, consent-gated, sovereign | Local node stewards |
| **Commitment pool** | Circulation — offers and needs become routable capacity | Community pooling |
| **Hub Cultivator** | Redistribution — trust-based capital allocation | Landscape stewards |
| **TBFF** | Redistribution — algorithmic threshold-based overflow | Participant-set thresholds, on-chain |
| **AI agents** | Navigation — graph traversal and matchmaking | Agents serve the commons |
| **Federation** | Scale — local pools connect across bioregions | KOI-net protocol, node-governed |
| **Bioregional Finance** | Capital — connects global investment to ground-level regenerators | BFFs: Trust, Studio, Investment Co., Bank |

The minimum viable version is just the top two layers: run the workshop, structure the outputs, put them in a knowledge graph. Everything else is composable on top, adopted as the community is ready.

The maximum version is a living system — a mycelial network of commitment pools, federated across bioregions, navigable by AI agents, governed by communities that trust each other enough to share what they have and ask for what they need, and capitalized by Bioregional Financing Facilities that direct investment toward demonstrated regeneration capacity.

The distance between minimum and maximum is not a technical gap. It's a trust gap, closed workshop by workshop, commitment by commitment, season by season. And the Bioregional Bank that one day issues a nature-based currency pegged to salmon abundance in the Salish Sea begins with one person in a room in Victoria saying: "I can offer twenty hours of Garry Oak habitat restoration."
